## Abstract

This paper gives the geometry layer of the series. The complex critical-line variable \(1/2+it\) is lifted to quaternionic slices
$$
q_{\mathbf u}(t)=\frac12+\mathbf u t,\qquad \mathbf u^2=-1.
$$
The classical line is the slice \(\mathbf u=i\). We separate affine slice location from unit-phase closure \(Q=e^{\mathbf u\phi}\), and record the six-step cadence \(Q^3=-1,\;Q^6=1\) when \(\operatorname{Re}(Q)=1/2\). This cadence is geometric intuition only. No proof of RH is claimed.

## Reader Guide

## 1. Quaternionic slices and critical hyperslice

Let \(\mathbf u\) be a unit imaginary quaternion (\(\mathbf u^2=-1\)). Define
$$
\mathbb C_{\mathbf u}=\{a+\mathbf u b:\;a,b\in\mathbb R\}.
$$
The quaternionic critical hyperslice is
$$
\Sigma_{1/2}=\{q\in\mathbb H:\operatorname{Re}(q)=1/2\},
$$
with affine slice family
$$
q_{\mathbf u}(t)=\frac12+\mathbf u t.
$$
For \(\mathbf u=i\), this is exactly the classical critical line \(1/2+it\).

## 2. Affine slice versus unit phase

Keep two objects distinct:

- Affine slice location: \(q=1/2+\mathbf u t\).
- Unit phase motion: \(Q=e^{\mathbf u\phi}=\cos\phi+\mathbf u\sin\phi\).

The first is additive geometry; the second is multiplicative cadence.

## 3. Six-step cadence as geometric intuition

If \(\operatorname{Re}(Q)=1/2\), then \(\cos\phi=1/2\), so \(\phi=\pi/3\) modulo \(2\pi\). Hence
$$
Q^3=-1,\qquad Q^6=1.
$$
This cadence helps visualize closure at real part \(1/2\), but the main bridge to mass shells comes through the eigenvalue operator in Paper 4.

## 4. Quaternionic lift of the prime phase and residual

The critical-line prime route lifts slice-wise as
$$
p^{-(1/2+\mathbf u t)}=p^{-1/2}e^{-\mathbf u t\log p}.
$$
If
$$
\Xi(t)=\xi\!\left(\frac12+it\right),
$$
a compatible slice lift is
$$
\xi_{\mathbb H}(1/2+\mathbf u t)=\operatorname{Re}\Xi(t)+\mathbf u\,\operatorname{Im}\Xi(t),
$$
with
$$
\|\xi_{\mathbb H}(1/2+\mathbf u t)\|^2=|\Xi(t)|^2.
$$
So the same complex spectral trace data can be read across quaternionic orientation slices.
