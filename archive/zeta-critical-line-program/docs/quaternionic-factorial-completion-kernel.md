# Quaternionic Factorial as the Completion Kernel

## What the Quaternionic Factorial is
The Quaternionic Factorial is the baseline completion layer
\[
◎(q)=\int_0^\infty e^{-x}x^q\,dx=\Gamma(q+1),
\]
with slice convention \(x^q=e^{q\log x}\).
For a slice \(q=a+\mathbf u t\), \(\mathbf u^2=-1\), we use
\[
◎(a+\mathbf u t)=\operatorname{Re}\Gamma(a+1+it)+\mathbf u\,\operatorname{Im}\Gamma(a+1+it).
\]

## Why Gamma completion matters
Raw prime phase routes carry oscillatory structure, but completion terms determine analytic weighting and curvature across scale. In this series, that role is called the completion envelope.

## Where \(◎\) fits in the paper series
The series uses a three-layer map:
\[
\text{phase}\rightarrow\text{completion}\rightarrow\text{norm},
\]
\[
p^{-q}\rightarrow ◎(q)\rightarrow P\bar P.
\]
Paper 2 introduces \(◎\) as a slice-lifted Gamma block, Paper 4 treats it as the completion kernel in the completed closure selector, and Paper 5 places dressed variants in calibration as candidate selection mechanisms.

## Difference between \(◎\) and AGQF
- Baseline \(◎\): structural completion envelope.
- Dressed forms (AGQF): baseline completion multiplied by a fixed prefactor to introduce testable node/selection behavior.

AGQF is not the core completion layer; it is a calibration-side proposal.

## Non-claims
- \(◎\) does not by itself generate zeta zeros.
- Gamma has no zeros, so completion alone is not cancellation.
- AGQF selection is not yet a validated physical law.
- The zeta–mass bridge remains conditional until \(L_\ast\) and any selection rules are independently fixed.
- No proof of RH.
- No Standard Model mass derivation.
